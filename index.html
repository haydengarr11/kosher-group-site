Description

The Kosher Diet is a food diet that follows regulations for food consumption and food preparation methods. Not cooking or consuming meat and dairy together is a prevalent activity that insiders partake in when following the Kosher Diet. Individuals can choose to follow the Kosher Diet for any reason that motivates them, and some examples of this are health benefits, environmental benefits, and religious or cultural reasons. When preparing meals food products that contain dairy canâ€™t be cooked using the same utensils. There needs to be separate plates, silverware, pots, pans, and any other cooking or eating utensil. During the clean up portion of the food preparation process it is recommended to have separate sinks to wash utensils that have touched dairy and separate utensils for foods that have touched meat products. If this is not possible, preventing any dishes from touching the sink and then having separate drying racks is ideal.
          When consuming meat and dairy products, the mouth should be rinsed out with water, solid food or liquid should be consumed, and hands should be washed and cleaned. Insiders following the Kosher Diet can have a wait time in between consuming meat and dairy products that varies. However, the most common wait time is 6 hours. There are guidelines are the kinds of meats that can be eaten as well. Examples of meats forbidden to be consumed include pig and camel. Examples of meats that are allowed for consumption include deer, cattle, sheep, goats and bison. However, there are restrictions on how these animals can be slaughtered and prepared as well to follow Kosher guidelines.

Research Question

How does cooking and consuming meat and dairy separately function for insiders following the Kosher Diet?

Interpretive Thesis

For insiders, cooking and consuming meat and dairy separately to follow the Kosher Diet functions as a ritual generating a sacred, unseen order by ordering time and space creating a hierophany.
